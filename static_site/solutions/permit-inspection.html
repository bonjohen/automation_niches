Permit & Inspection Coordination for Contractors

Track permits across multiple jurisdictions and coordinate inspection schedules automatically. Eliminate delays from missed deadlines and stalled approvals.

Pain Points

  • Complex, multi-agency coordination: Contractors deal with building departments, zoning boards, environmental agencies, fire marshals, and utilities - each with separate applications and schedules
  • Manual tracking leads to missed deadlines: Without automated reminders, required inspections might be scheduled late or forgotten, causing costly rework or stop-work orders
  • Jurisdictional variations: What's required for a permit in one city can be entirely different in the next - keeping up with varying codes, forms, and fees is nearly impossible manually
  • High volume of paperwork and human error: Permit applications involve extensive forms and documentation. A typo or forgetting a required attachment can derail a permit for weeks
  • Reactive problem-solving: Without real-time tracking, contractors discover issues only when a problem arises, causing project delays

Typical Workflow Today

  • A project manager or expeditor submits permit applications using each authority's process - online portals, emailed PDFs, or in-person paper submissions
  • After submission, they track status by periodically checking each city's portal, calling the permit office, or using a spreadsheet or whiteboard
  • Inspection scheduling is usually done by the site superintendent who keeps a paper log or simple calendar of required inspections
  • If a permit has prerequisites (like health department approval before building permit), the onus is on the contractor to remember and chase those in parallel
  • Project teams often find themselves reacting: "Why hasn't the permit been issued? Oh, there was a fee invoice we missed in the portal"

Why It's Hard

  • Jurisdiction overload: A contractor might deal with a dozen different cities/counties, each with its own website, forms, and quirks
  • Disjointed communication: There's typically no single point of truth - some info comes via email, some via posted permit boards, some via phone
  • Volume of minor tasks: The permit process has many small tasks that are error-prone: pay this fee, call this inspector, pick up the physical permit
  • Delays directly impact cost: Every day a permit is delayed or an inspection can't be scheduled is a day of project delay - time equals money
  • Regulatory complexity and risk: Missing a required inspection or proceeding without proper permits can lead to legal fines or having to tear out work

How Automation Helps

  • Central permit tracking system: A single dashboard for all permits across all jurisdictions showing every permit's status, required steps, and associated documents
  • Automated workflow routing: When a permit application is submitted, the software automatically routes documents to the right internal and external parties
  • Customized checklists and requirements library: Maintain a database of permitting requirements by jurisdiction and project type - automatically generate checklists when starting a new project
  • Deadline reminders and alerts: The software actively monitors all deadlines and sends automated alerts to the responsible person before due dates
  • Inspection scheduling and integration: Automatically schedule inspections in the optimal order when prerequisites are met and consolidate all upcoming inspections in one view
  • Real-time status tracking and early issue detection: Real-time dashboards flag delays as they happen - if an application has been "in plan review" longer than typical, the system alerts you
  • Document management and version control: All permit-related documents are stored in one place with audit trails - field teams can access the latest approved plans on a tablet
  • Automatic updates with regulatory changes: Some advanced systems tie into code libraries to update checklists when a city introduces new permit requirements
  • Resource optimization and transparency: Know exactly when each inspection will happen, allowing better planning of crews and resources
