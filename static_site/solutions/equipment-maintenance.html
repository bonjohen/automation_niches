Equipment Maintenance Record & Warranty Tracking

Automate preventive maintenance scheduling and warranty expiration alerts. Reduce downtime, extend equipment life, and never miss a warranty claim opportunity.

Pain Points

  • Scattered maintenance records: Many SMBs keep equipment maintenance logs on paper or in basic spreadsheets attached to each site or machine - leading to disorganized or incomplete records
  • Missed service intervals and reactive fixes: Without automation, maintenance is often reactive - run to failure - because tracking exact service intervals (hours/mileage since last service) manually is cumbersome
  • Forgotten warranty expirations: Equipment typically comes with manufacturer warranties (1 year, 3 years, etc.) - manually remembering those deadlines is hard, so companies often miss the window to get free repairs
  • Warranty claim headaches: Even when within warranty, proving that maintenance was done per the warranty requirements can be challenging if records are poor - leading to denied claims
  • Asset information silos: Key information about equipment (purchase date, vendor, warranty terms, serial numbers) is often buried in purchase files or emails - without centralized data, recalls or service bulletins are missed

Typical Workflow Today

  • Maintenance scheduling might rely on a simple calendar or whiteboard in a workshop - "Truck #5 - oil change due in March" - if disciplined, they update a logbook when it's done
  • Warranty info usually sits in a file (physical or PDF in a folder) - the purchase date and warranty period might be noted in an Excel sheet if someone created one, but typically no proactive alerts
  • When equipment breaks, someone will search for the purchase paperwork to see if it's under warranty - this could involve rifling through emails or asking accounting for the original invoice date
  • For each repair or maintenance action, small shops might have carbon-copy books or individual service reports that are kept in a folder on the machine or in a drawer
  • There's often no alert for warranty claims deadlines - some warranties require notifying the manufacturer within a certain time of failure, which is easily overlooked

Why It's Hard

  • Multiple assets, multiple schedules: A business could have dozens of pieces of equipment, each with its own maintenance interval (hours-based, date-based, usage-based) - manually tracking each one requires constant vigilance
  • Interdepartmental gaps: Maintenance might be handled by an ops team, while warranty and purchases are handled by finance or procurement - without a unified system, the people fixing equipment might not think about warranty status
  • Human forgetfulness: Warranty expiration dates are often 1-5 years out - humans are notoriously bad at remembering events that far ahead unless there's a system of reminders
  • Administrative burden: Logging maintenance manually (writing details of work done, parts used) is extra work technicians might skip when they're hustling to get equipment running again
  • Unplanned downtime & costs: The consequence of poor tracking is unplanned breakdowns that disrupt operations and cost more (overtime, rush parts) - the pain is felt sporadically during breakdowns rather than predictably
  • Lack of data for decision-making: Without good records, it's hard to answer "Is it time to replace this machine?" or "Which equipment is costing us the most in repairs?"

How Automation Helps

  • CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System) for scheduling: An automated system keeps an inventory of all equipment with attributes like last service date, service interval, and warranty period - it automatically generates work orders or reminders when maintenance is due
  • Digital maintenance logs: Instead of paper, technicians use a tablet or computer to record what they did, parts used, and any notes - all history is stored under each equipment's profile, building a comprehensive service history
  • Warranty tracking and alerts: The system records purchase date and warranty terms for each asset - it can alert you 60 or 90 days before a warranty expires so you can schedule a thorough inspection or address known issues while still covered
  • Integrated inventory and parts management: Advanced systems also track spare parts and link with maintenance - they can automatically suggest which warranty parts to use or identify recall-affected equipment by serial number
  • Analytics and lifecycle insights: With all maintenance and cost data in one place, the system can provide reports like "Maintenance cost per year per machine" or "Downtime hours per asset" - helping identify equipment that's approaching end-of-life
  • Auto-populated warranty claims: When something breaks under warranty, the system has all the info needed: serial, purchase date, maintenance logs as proof - some platforms can even generate warranty claim forms or emails pre-filled
  • Reminder for post-warranty service plans: The system might also remind you when an OEM maintenance plan is ending or if calibration certificates for instruments expire, aligning with warranty timelines
  • Improved uptime and cost savings: By automating maintenance schedules and warranty tracking, equipment reliability improves - catching repairs under warranty saves direct costs on parts/labor
  • Documentation for resale: If you sell used equipment, having a full maintenance and repair log with proof of warranty adherence can increase resale value
